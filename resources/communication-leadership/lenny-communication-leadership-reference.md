## Communication & Storytelling

### Andy Raskin's Strategic Narrative (5 steps)
- Name a big, relevant change in the world
- Show there'll be winners and losers
- Tease the "promised land" (happy ending for customer)
- Introduce your product as the "magic gift" that helps get there
- Show evidence you can make the story come true
- *Examples: Salesforce, Zuora, Gong*

### Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle
- Lead with the conclusion/recommendation
- Support with 2-3 key arguments
- Back each argument with data
- *Used by McKinsey, recommended by Wes Kao*

### Minto Pyramid / SCR Framework
- Situation → Complication → Resolution
- Structures communication for executives who skim

### Nancy Duarte's "What Is / What Could Be" (Pumpkin Teeth)
Source: `podcasts/nancy-duarte.md`

- Alternate between current state ("what is") and future possibility ("what could be")
- Creates contrast and longing for the future state
- Peak at "new bliss" — the desired end state
- Your audience is the hero, not you
- Infuse talks with 3-act story structure (fires brains in same order — fMRI evidence)

### April Dunford's Positioning Framework (5 components)
- Competitive alternatives
- Unique attributes
- Value for customer
- Target customer characteristics
- Market category

### Matt Abrahams' Speaking Toolkit
Source: podcast episode

- Anxiety management techniques for public speaking
- On-the-spot structures: PREP (Point, Reason, Example, Point) and What/So What/Now What

### Matthew Dicks' 7 Humor Strategies
- Tactical approaches to create surprise and connection in storytelling

### Tara Seshan's Tradeoff Communication
- OSR Framework with repetition and steelmanning
- How to communicate tradeoffs so leaders will listen

### Wes Kao's Communication Frameworks
Source: `podcasts/wes-kao.md`

- **Super Specific How** — most writers spend too much time on what/why, not enough on how. Cut backstory, get to actionable specifics
- **Content Hierarchy of BS** — short formats have more room for BS; long-form accountability increases
- **"Start Right Before You Get Eaten by the Bear"** — cut preamble, get to the juicy part
- **State Change Method** — framework for running better meetings
- **Managing Up** — embrace managing your boss; they'll appreciate you more

## Influence & Persuasion

### Jules Walter's 5 Influence Tactics
Source: `newsletters/a-pms-guide-to-influence.md`

1. **Seek Intel on Decision-Making** — understand what stakeholders value, who they consult, what they fear
   - Ask: What are your team's top goals and OKRs? What's your process for deciding what to prioritize? Who are the key decision makers?
2. **Frame Your Message from Their POV** — use their language, address their goals
   - Example: Pitching monetization at Slack to Stewart Butterfield — reframed around user value-creation, not revenue
3. **"The Meeting Before the Meeting"** — prime both detractors AND champions before critical meetings
   - Meet opponents to understand objections
   - Secure champion support for key points
4. **Make People Feel Heard and Validated** — playback: "What I'm hearing is..."
   - People won't listen until they feel truly understood
5. **Manage the Clock** — state desired outcomes upfront, control meeting flow
   - Different audiences need different approaches (skip context for fast processors, use memos for detailed readers)

### Lenny's 7 Influence Strategies
Source: `newsletters/how-to-get-better-at-influence.md`

1. **Make Their Goals Your Goals** — frame your ask as helping them hit their goals
   - Example: Airbnb Superhost badge — reframed from host retention to guest conversion for search team
2. **Charge Your Trust Battery** — trust is a gradient, not binary (Tobi Lutke concept)
   - Do what you say, listen, create reciprocity, credit others, take feedback seriously
3. **Help Them See What You See** — Covey's "slow is fast"; don't rush decisions, loop in early, explain the why
4. **Show Success** — credibility compounds. Amazon: "Leaders are right, a lot."
5. **Bring Evidence** — priority order: hard data > synthesized research > anecdotes > competitive analysis > similar examples
   - Airbnb review system change: convinced through double-blind studies from other fields
6. **Leverage Authority** — gain support from influential people; credibility increases when others vouch for you
7. **Be Likable** — Cialdini + Carnegie: smile, ask about their life, give compliments, recognize success publicly, criticize privately

### Shivani Berry's Buy-In Tactics
Source: `newsletters/getting-buy-in.md`

1. **Co-Creation** — collaborate early with stakeholders using straw-man proposals; creates investment and ownership
2. **Presence** — stop saying "sorry" when unwarranted; eliminate qualifiers ("just," "actually," "quickly"); own your voice
3. **Storytelling** — focus on why stakeholders should care; cut backstory; talk like you're explaining to a teenager

### Managing Up (5 tactics)
Source: `newsletters/managing-up.md`

1. **Over-communicate** — weekly "State of The Me" emails: current priorities, things on your mind, blockers
2. **Set Realistic Expectations** — performance judged against expectations, not just results
3. **Be Proactive** — anticipate asks and problems; develop plans before being asked
4. **Prioritize + Communicate** — when new work comes, update priorities with manager, discuss tradeoffs
5. **Do Great Work** — better performance = more trust = more freedom and influence

### Influence in Large Orgs
Source: `newsletters/this-week-9-breaking-into-growth-leading-with-influence-and-not-stepping-on-toes.md`

- **Find the decision-maker** and focus efforts there
- **Ask directly**: "What would it take to convince you?"
- **Make the ask crystal clear** — concrete enough to action
- **Help them see priority** — why this matters above other work
- **Set a deadline** — creates urgency
- For ambiguous ownership: communicate then proceed; impact > ownership (Uber's "Meritocracy and Toe-Stepping")

### Key Psychological References
- Robert Cialdini — "Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion"
- Dale Carnegie — "How to Win Friends and Influence People"
- Stephen Covey — "slow is fast" principle

## Leadership

### Julie Zhuo (VP Design, Meta)
Source: `podcasts/julie-zhuo.md`

- Imposter syndrome is normal even at VP level (felt it 7-8 years)
- Growth comes from uncomfortable situations; embrace being in over your head
- Writing as clarification tool — approach writing as "letters to myself"
- Ask for help, be vulnerable, share what you find hard
- Different management approaches needed for new grads vs senior people
- Book: "The Making of a Manager"

### Claire Hughes Johnson (COO Stripe, VP Google)
Source: `podcasts/claire-hughes-johnson.md`

**Four Operating Principles (from "Scaling People"):**
1. Self-awareness to build mutual awareness
2. Say the thing you think you cannot say
3. Distinguish between management and leadership
4. Come back to operating system

**House Metaphor for Scaling:**
- Founding documents (mission, long-term goals, operating principles)
- Supporting structures (quarterly reviews, OKRs, planning)
- Mechanicals (operating cadence/rhythm)

**Operating Principles Crystallization:**
1. Identify core values
2. Narrow to top 3 under pressure
3. Find story behind each value
4. Determine work style tendencies
5. Document as operating system

- Explorer, not lecturer — use hypothesis-based coaching
- Fewer titles, more flexibility; career ladders before 200-300 people
- Leadership creates vision; management enables people

### Kim Scott (Radical Candor)
Source: `podcasts/kim-scott.md`

**The 2x2 Matrix:**
- Challenge directly + Care personally = **Radical Candor** (ideal)
- Challenge directly - Care personally = Obnoxious aggression
- Care personally - Challenge directly = **Ruinous empathy** (most common mistake, 90% of leaders)
- Neither = Manipulative insincerity

**HHIIPPP Feedback Principles:**
Humble, Helpful, Immediate, In-person/Synchronous, Praise public, Criticize private, Personality-neutral

**CORE Feedback Structure:**
Context → Observation → Result → Next step

**Career Conversations:** Three 45-minute conversations about past, future, and dreams

### Ben Horowitz
Source: `podcasts/ben-horowitz.md`

- Decision-making when both options are terrible — leadership is choosing the slightly better option
- "Run toward fear rather than away from it"
- Hesitation kills teams — psychological muscle is clicking in the abyss

### Jerry Colonna (CEO Coach)
Source: `podcasts/jerry-colonna.md`

**Leadership Equation:** Practical skills + Radical self-inquiry + Shared experiences = Enhanced leadership + Greater resilience

**Four Radical Self-Inquiry Questions:**
1. What am I not saying that I need to say?
2. What am I saying that's not being heard?
3. What's being said that I'm not hearing?
4. How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?

### Camille Fournier (CTO Rent the Runway)
Source: `podcasts/camille-fournier.md`

**PM-Engineer Friction Sources:**

- Not understanding details

- Hoarding ideas and creative input

- Don't measure infinitely — at some point you have to cut
- Treat engineers like adults; give them real hard problems and hold them accountable

### Brian Halligan (HubSpot co-founder, Sequoia CEO Coach)
Source: `podcasts/brian-halligan.md`

### Turning Around an Underperforming Team
Source: `newsletters/turning-around-an-underperforming-team---issue-17.md`

1. Set clear expectations with manager (6-12 months of reduced impact)
2. Set high bar for quality — don't settle for good enough
3. Motivate by connecting changes to personal motivations (impact, promotion, work-life balance)
4. **Observe-Identify-Share-Act** framework (from Nonviolent Communication)
5. Find allies — understand who's aligned, who disagrees, who's unsure

### Org Design
Source: `newsletters/general-management-functional-and-hybrid-models-which-org-design-works-best-for.md`

**GM vs Functional vs Hybrid — decision factors:**
1. How close is North Star metric to revenue?
2. How critical is holistic customer journey?
- GM model: multiple product groups, predictable outcomes, significant scale
- Functional model: early stage, product cohesion needed, indirect revenue
- Hybrid: most common in practice

### Patrick Winston's "How to Speak" (MIT)
Source: [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Unzc731iCUY)

**Communication Quality Formula:** K (knowledge) x P (practice) >> T (talent) — knowledge and practice beat natural talent

**Opening — the Empowerment Promise:**
- Don't open with a joke (audience isn't ready yet)
- Open with a promise: "By the end of this hour, you will know X that you didn't before"
- This gives the audience a reason to stay

**Four Presentation Heuristics:**
1. **Cycle** — circle back to key ideas 3x (at any moment ~20% of audience is zoned out)
2. **Build a fence** — distinguish your idea from similar ones so they don't blur together
3. **Verbal punctuation** — provide signposts/numbering so lost listeners can re-engage
4. **Ask questions** — pause ~7 seconds for answers; calibrate difficulty (not too easy, not too hard)

**Time & Place:**
- Best time: 11am (people are awake, not post-lunch)
- Lights ON always — dim lights = sleep signal
- Scout the venue beforehand
- Room should be >50% full (empty room kills energy)

**Tools — Blackboard vs Props vs Slides:**
- Blackboard/whiteboard for *teaching* (graphic quality, speed-matched to comprehension, gives hands something to do)
- Props create empathic mirroring — audience feels themselves doing the action (can't replicate with slides)
- Slides for *presenting/exposing*, not teaching

**Props & physical demonstration (Winston's most memorable examples):**
- **Bicycle wheel (via Seymour Papert):** mechanical engineers use right-hand rule and get gyroscopic precession right ~50% of the time. Put duct tape on one segment, think about just that piece — you'll never get it wrong. The prop teaches "look at the problem the right way" AND is the thing people remember decades later
- **Pendulum (Alan Lazarus, MIT 8.01):** professor puts his head against the wall, releases a heavy steel ball on a long wire from his nose, waits several seconds while it swings back and returns to gently kiss his nose. "You have many seconds to think: this guy really believes in the conservation of energy." Don't try at home — humans instinctively push
- **Serbia convent:** a nun pulled Winston's hands out from behind his back — in that culture, hands behind your back implies concealing a weapon. Blackboards/whiteboards solve the "what do I do with my hands" problem by giving them a job
- **Seymour Papert pointing:** Winston watched his lecture twice — once for content, once for style. Discovered Papert constantly pointed at the board, but none of it related to what he was saying. Still an effective technique
- **Jim Glass remembering the broken pointer:** "I remember that talk — that's the one where you broke the pointer." Props are disproportionately what people remember, even 20 years later
- *Meta-lesson: Winston's own talk is a masterclass in what he teaches. Each anecdote above IS a prop — concrete, physical, empathically mirrored. He doesn't just explain that props work; the stories themselves function as props in his lecture*

**Slide Rules:**
- Too many slides, too much text (universal problem)
- Remove: background images, logos, titles, bullet points
- Minimum font size: 40-50pt (smaller = temptation to overload)
- "Slides are condiments to what you're saying, not the main event — or the opposite way around"
- Never read your slides aloud (audience is literate; reading competes with listening — one language processor)
- Stand near the screen, not far away (no tennis-match head-turning)
- Never use laser pointers (forces you to turn your back; kills eye contact). Use arrows on slides instead
- Print slides on table to check for "heaviness" — if too dense, simplify
- **Hapax legomenon** — one intentionally complex/overwhelming slide per talk to make a point about complexity itself

**Inspiring People:**
- Beginners respond to: "you can do it" (confidence)
- Experts respond to: new ways of seeing familiar problems
- Everyone responds to: genuine passion for the subject
- Express enthusiasm early, during the empowerment promise

**Teaching = Storytelling:**
- Humans are storytelling animals — thinking IS story manipulation
- Teaching how to think = providing stories, questions to ask about them, methods to analyze them, ways to assemble and evaluate them

**Practice:**
- Practice with people who DON'T know your work — colleagues and advisors who know your topic will "hallucinate" content that isn't actually in your presentation
- Tell practice friends: "If you can't make me cry, I won't value you as a friend anymore"

**Meta-principle — build your own armamentarium:**
- Watch speakers you admire, ask yourself WHY they're effective
- Build your own personal repertoire and style — don't copy someone else's
- "It's a very non-linear process. You never know when it's going to happen" — one technique from your toolkit might be the one that makes the difference

**Job Talk / Persuasion (5-minute rule):**
- Within 5 minutes you must show: (1) a vision someone cares about, and (2) that you've done something
- Vision = problem people care about + what's different about your approach
- List the steps needed to realize the vision, show which you've completed
- End with a "contributions" slide

**Why packaging matters (Julia Child anecdote):**
- "Is it fun to be famous?" — "You get used to it. But you know what occurred to me? You never get used to being ignored."
- Your ideas are like your children — you don't want them to go into the world in rags

**Winston's Star — how to make work memorable (all start with S):**
1. **Symbol** — a visual icon for the idea (e.g., the arch)
2. **Slogan** — a short phrase that encapsulates the work ("one-shot learning")
3. **Surprise** — the counterintuitive element
4. **Salient idea** — one idea that stands out above the rest ("near miss")
5. **Story** — how you did it, how it works, why it matters

**Closing — what NOT to do:**
- Don't end with a collaborators slide (put collaborators on slide 1)
- Don't end with "Questions?" or "Thank You" or "The End" — wastes your final impression
- Don't end with "conclusions" — nobody cares about conclusions; they care about *contributions*

**Closing — what TO do:**
- End with a joke (audience is now warmed up; they'll feel the whole talk was fun)
- Or end with a salute to the audience (acknowledge what makes them special)
- Don't say "thank you" as your final words — it implies they stayed out of politeness
- Contribution slide should be your last visible slide (what people see during Q&A)

## Cross-Cutting Principles

### Writing as Leadership Tool
- Clarifies thinking (Julie Zhuo)
- Forces deep engagement with ideas
- Creates alignment artifacts
- Forces concision and clarity

### Trust-Building Mechanisms
- Do what you say (consistency)
- Listen actively
- Create reciprocity
- Understand others' incentives and goals
- Share the credit

### Decision-Maker Strategy
- Identify who actually decides
- Understand their values, fears, goals
- Frame proposals through their lens
- Pre-work with supporters and detractors
